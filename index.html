User Research — Understanding Health Anxiety
Interviewed 12 participants across 3 groups: parents with young children, adults managing chronic conditions, elderly users. Ran contextual inquiry watching how people actually searched for health information — Google, Reddit, WebMD — and mapped the emotional journey from symptom to decision.
Key insight: People do not want a diagnosis. They want someone to say "I hear you, here is what this probably means, here is when to worry." The AI had to feel like a calm, informed friend — not a medical database.
Affinity map — pain point clusters
n=12 · 3 user groups · contextual inquiry + semi-structured interviews
3 Competing Design Directions — Before Committing
Before any wireframe I explored three fundamentally different approaches. Each was prototyped and shown to 4 users each.
Direction A — Symptom checker
❌ Rejected — felt clinical and cold. No emotional acknowledgement.
Direction B — Urgency triage first
❌ Rejected — front-loading urgency caused anxiety before understanding.
Direction C — Conversational ✓ Chosen
✓ Warm, open, trust layered in. Human before clinical.
Wireframe Iterations — Chat UI
14 conversation states mapped. 3 major UI iterations before the final confidence framing pattern emerged.
v1 — No trust signals (rejected)
❌ Users felt dismissed. No trust framing, no safety messaging.
v3 — Confidence + disclaimer ✓ Final
✓ Users felt MORE informed and LESS anxious. Core trust pattern established.
Prototype — 14 Conversation States
3 rounds of usability testing with 8 participants each. Core flows: symptom input, follow-up questions, urgency detection, doctor referral.
State 1 — Consent & onboarding
Trust-first. HIPAA disclosure before any health data entered.
State 7 — AI response + confidence
Confidence level + disclaimer on every response. Core trust pattern.
State 14 — Doctor referral
Clear escalation path. Never leaves user without a next step.

Research — Why Kids Drop Out of Coding
Reviewed spaced repetition research and studied Scratch, Code.org, Duolingo. Ran concept testing with 6 kids across two age groups, watching where they disengaged. Interviewed 4 parents about what they actually wanted their kids to learn.
Core tension: Games are designed to be frictionless. Learning requires productive friction. The design had to find the exact point where challenge becomes engaging rather than frustrating — and that point differs for an 8-year-old vs a 16-year-old.
Competitive analysis — engagement vs real code
Gap identified: high engagement AND real code. No product in that quadrant for kids.
3 Game UX Approaches — Why Dungeon RPG Won
A — Linear course map
❌ Rejected — felt like homework. Kids didn't care about progression.
B — Open world map
❌ Rejected — younger users (8-10) disengaged within 90 seconds.
C — Dungeon RPG ✓ Chosen
✓ Linear (can't get lost), narrative (code has purpose), real syntax.
