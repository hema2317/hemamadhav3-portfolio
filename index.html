User Research — Understanding Health Anxiety
Interviewed 12 participants across 3 groups: parents with young children, adults managing chronic conditions, and elderly users. Ran contextual inquiry sessions watching how people actually searched for health info — Google, Reddit, WebMD — and mapped the emotional journey from symptom to decision.
87% felt more anxious after Googling symptoms Users want acknowledgement before information Trust built through specificity, not reassurance Elderly users needed 3× more confirmation steps Mobile-first: 91% searched on phone at night
Key insight
People do not want a diagnosis. They want someone to say "I hear you, here is what this probably means, here is when to worry." The AI had to feel like a calm, informed friend — not a medical database.
Affinity map — pain point clusters
n=12 participants · 3 user groups · contextual inquiry + interviews
Three Competing Design Directions
Before committing to any solution I explored three fundamentally different approaches. Each had real trade-offs weighed against user research and HIPAA constraints.
Direction A — Symptom Checker
❌ Rejected — felt like filling out a form. Cold, clinical, no emotional acknowledgement.
Direction B — Urgency Triage First
❌ Rejected — front-loading urgency caused anxiety before understanding. Defeated the purpose.
Direction C — Conversational ✓ Chosen
✓ Chosen — warm, open, structured guidance layered in. Human before clinical.
Wireframe Iterations — Chat UI
Mapped 14 distinct conversation states. Wireframed three iterations of the core chat UI before settling on the final trust pattern.
v1 — No trust signals (rejected)
❌ Users felt dismissed. Response felt robotic, no trust framing, no safety messaging.
v3 — Confidence + disclaimer ✓ Final
✓ Users reported feeling MORE informed and LESS anxious. Confidence indicator became core pattern.
Iteration learning
v1 tested badly — users felt dismissed. v2 added disclaimers but felt legalistic. v3 added confidence framing — showing the AI's certainty level. In testing, this made users feel more informed, not less. It became the core trust pattern throughout the app.
Prototype — Key Interaction States
14 conversation states prototyped in Figma. Three rounds of usability testing with 8 participants each. Core flows tested: symptom input, follow-up questions, urgency detection, and doctor referral.
State 1 — Consent & onboarding
Trust-first onboarding. HIPAA disclosure before any health data is entered.
State 7 — AI response with confidence
Confidence level + disclaimer on every AI response. Core trust pattern.
State 14 — Doctor referral
Clear escalation path. Never leaves user without a next step.

Research — Why Kids Drop Out of Coding
Reviewed spaced repetition research and studied Scratch, Code.org, and Duolingo. Ran concept testing with 6 kids across two age groups, watching where they disengaged. Interviewed 4 parents about what they wanted their kids to actually learn.
Kids disengaged when feedback delayed 2+ seconds Narrative context made abstract code feel purposeful Parents wanted visible progress, not just fun Teens (13–16) wanted to feel like real developers Younger kids needed story before syntax
Core tension to resolve
Games are designed to be frictionless. Learning requires productive friction. The design had to find the exact point where challenge becomes engaging rather than frustrating — and that point differs for an 8-year-old vs a 16-year-old.
Competitive analysis
Gap identified: high engagement AND real code. No product in that quadrant for kids.
Three Game UX Approaches — Why Dungeon RPG Won
A — Linear course map
❌ Rejected — felt like filling out a form. No emotional stakes, kids didn't care about progression.
B — Open world map
❌ Rejected — tested poorly with 8–10 year olds who felt lost without direction.
C — Dungeon RPG ✓ Chosen
✓ Chosen — linear progression (can't get lost), narrative purpose (code defeats monsters), real syntax.
Wireframes — AI Hint System & Spaced Repetition
3-level hint escalation system
Kids never just get the answer — hints escalate progressively. Learning stays productive.
Spaced repetition curriculum loop
Every concept is introduced, practised, reviewed 24h later, then tested in a boss battle before levelling up.

Research — The Natural Skincare Trust Gap
Interviewed 18 women aged 25–55 before formulating a single product. Key finding: customers wanted botanical products but had been burned by empty "natural" claims repeatedly. Trust had to be earned through specificity, science, and honesty — not marketing.
87% had bought ineffective "natural" products Research citations increased purchase intent 3× Before/after photos were #1 trust signal Ingredient science needed to feel accessible Price sensitivity vs quality — clear positioning needed
Core design principle
Every product page had to earn trust through specificity: real research citations, real before/after photos, ingredient science in plain English. No vague claims. The UX research was hidden behind a toggle — shoppers see a clean product page, recruiters see full process.
Trust hierarchy — page architecture
Trust architecture: most credibility-building content appears first. Research last (and hidden by default).
Brand Identity System
Brand system rooted in Ayurvedic aesthetics — gold as the primary accent (traditionally associated with purification in Ayurveda), Cormorant Garamond for classical elegance, warm cream palette that feels botanical. Every visual element connects to the product ingredients.
Color & type system
Formula development — v1 → v3

UX System — Recruiter Toggle Pattern
Solved a real product tension: shoppers want a clean, beautiful product page. Recruiters want to see deep UX process. One page serves both audiences through a hidden toggle design.
Shopper view — clean product page
Clean, benefit-focused. No process clutter. CTA visible above the fold.
